  • The concept of the neuron as a distinct unit was established by the Spanish pathologist Santiago Ramón y Cajal.

 The anatomy of the brain has been studied for over a century by slicing the brain and examining the different sections. These sections are made in different directions - horizontal (transverse), coronal, and sagittal - each providing a unique perspective of the brain's regions.

  • The brain's cortex is a layered structure, with each layer containing different types of neurons and connectivity. The layers are named based on the morphology and function of the neurons within them, with layer 4 generally serving as the input layer, receiving inputs from sub-cortical structures, mainly the thalamus.
  •  Structure of a neuron:-  which consists of a cell soma (body), dendrites, and an axon. The soma contains the nucleus and is responsible for maintaining the cell. The dendrites are protrusions that extend from the soma in multiple directions, while the axon is a thin structure that serves as the neuron's output. The axon can connect with multiple neurons, forming synapses, the points at which neurons connect to each other.

  • Theories of Camilo Golgi and Santiago Ramón y Cajal. Golgi proposed the reticular theory, suggesting that the nervous system was network-like, similar to the circulatory system. In contrast, Cajal proposed the neuron doctrine, suggesting that the neuron is a single entity and that neurons are connected in a discontinuous manner. The neuron doctrine was later confirmed with the advent of electron microscopy.

Week 1 - lecture 2:- Networks of neurons and synapses

  •  Structure of networks of neurons:- there are  100 billion neurons in the human brain.  Dendrites are the region where one neuron receives connections from other neurons.  The cell body or soma contains the nucleus and the other organelles necessary for cellular function.  The axon is a key component of nerve cells over which information is transmitted from one part of the neuron (e.g., the cell body) to the terminal regions of the neuron.  Axons can be rather long, extending up to a meter or so in some human sensory and motor nerve cells.  The synapse is the terminal region of the axon and it is here where one neuron forms a connection with another and conveys information through the process of synaptic transmission. One neuron can receive contacts from many different neurons.it has been estimated that one neuron can receive contacts from up to 10,000 other cells.  Consequently, the potential complexity of the networks is vast.  Similarly, any one neuron can contact up to 10,000 postsynaptic cells.

  • Role of synapses in facilitating these networks:-The places where neurons connect and communicate with each other are called synapses. Each neuron has anywhere between a few to hundreds of thousands of synaptic connections, and these connections can be with itself, neighboring neurons, or neurons in other regions of the brain.
  • Synapses are specialized junctions that allow communication between neurons in the nervous system. These connections are essential for transmitting information and signals within the brain and throughout the body. There are several types of synapses, each with its unique characteristics and functions. Types :- Chemical Synapses:
  • Chemical synapses are the most common type of synapse in the nervous system. They involve the release and reception of neurotransmitters, which are chemical messengers that transmit signals from one neuron to another. The process of signal transmission at chemical synapses can be divided into several steps
  • Electrical Synapses:

Electrical synapses, also known as gap junctions, are less common than chemical synapses but play essential roles in some parts of the nervous system. Unlike chemical synapses, electrical synapses allow for direct electrical communication between neurons. This is made possible by gap junctions, which are protein channels that connect the cytoplasm of adjacent neurons.

  • Concepts of synaptic plasticity :-Plasticity is the ability of the brain to change and adapt to new information. Synaptic plasticity is change that occurs at synapses, the junctions between neurons that allow them to communicate.The idea that synapses could change, and that this change depended on how active or inactive they were, was first proposed in 1949 by Canadian psychologist Donald Hebb.  It is a fundamental property of the nervous system and plays a crucial role in learning, memory, and overall brain function. There are two main types of synaptic plasticity.
  • Synaptic plasticity occurs in various brain regions and is not limited to specific types of neurons. It underlies various cognitive processes, including perception, attention, motor learning, and memory.

Synaptic transmission:-

Steps involved in synaptic transmission:-

  • Concept of neurotransmitters and their receptors at synapses:-

Neurotransmitters are chemical messengers that play a crucial role in communication between neurons at synapses. They are released by the presynaptic neuron and act on specific receptors located on the postsynaptic neuron, enabling the transmission of signals across the synapse. This process is fundamental to how the nervous system functions, as it allows neurons to relay information and coordinate various physiological and cognitive processes. Here's a more detailed explanation of neurotransmitters and their receptors at synapses:

 Neurotransmitters:

Neurotransmitters are a diverse group of chemicals that are synthesized and stored within the presynaptic neuron's vesicles. When an action potential reaches the presynaptic terminal, it triggers the release of these neurotransmitters into the synaptic cleft. The neurotransmitters then diffuse across the cleft and bind to specific receptors on the postsynaptic neuron. Some of the most common neurotransmitters include:

- **Glutamate:** The most abundant excitatory neurotransmitter in the brain, involved in promoting the generation of action potentials and enhancing synaptic transmission.

- **Gamma-Aminobutyric Acid (GABA):** The primary inhibitory neurotransmitter in the brain, involved in reducing neuronal activity and dampening excitatory signals.

- **Dopamine:** Plays a role in regulating motivation, reward, and pleasure, as well as motor control and various cognitive functions.

- **Serotonin:** Regulates mood, sleep, appetite, and other behavioral and physiological processes.

- **Acetylcholine:** Involved in muscle contraction at the neuromuscular junction and also functions as a neuromodulator in the brain, affecting attention, learning, and memory.

- **Norepinephrine (noradrenaline):** Influences arousal, alertness, and stress response, among other functions.

- **Epinephrine (adrenaline):** Primarily involved in the "fight or flight" response and stress-related reactions.

- **Endorphins:** Act as natural painkillers and are associated with feelings of pleasure and euphoria.

  • Spikes or action potentials

An action potential, also known as a spike or nerve impulse, is a brief and rapid electrical signal that travels along the membrane of a neuron. It is the fundamental means of communication between neurons and is responsible for transmitting information throughout the nervous system. Action potentials are crucial for various physiological processes, including sensory perception, motor control, and cognitive functions like memory and learning.

The generation and propagation of an action potential involve changes in the neuron's membrane potential, which is the difference in electrical charge between the inside and outside of the cell. The resting membrane potential of a neuron is usually negative, typically around -70 millivolts (mV), maintained by the selective permeability of the cell membrane to different ions, particularly sodium (Na+), potassium (K+), and chloride (Cl-) ions.

The process of an action potential can be divided into several stages:

1. **Resting State:** The neuron is at its resting membrane potential, with more negative charges inside the cell compared to the outside. Voltage-gated sodium (Na+) channels are closed, and voltage-gated potassium (K+) channels are mostly closed.

2. **Depolarization:** When the neuron receives a strong enough excitatory input, it depolarizes. Depolarization is the process of the membrane potential becoming less negative, and it occurs due to the opening of voltage-gated sodium channels in response to the incoming positive charges. Sodium ions rush into the cell, causing a rapid increase in the membrane potential.

3. **Threshold:** If the depolarization reaches a certain critical level, called the threshold, it triggers a positive feedback loop. At the threshold, a significant number of voltage-gated sodium channels open, leading to a massive influx of sodium ions, further depolarizing the membrane.

4. **Rising Phase:** The influx of sodium ions causes a sharp and rapid increase in the membrane potential. This positive feedback loop leads to the membrane potential rapidly reaching a peak value of around +40 mV.

5. **Falling Phase:** After reaching its peak, the voltage-gated sodium channels start to close, and voltage-gated potassium channels begin to open. The efflux of potassium ions from the cell leads to repolarization, restoring the negative membrane potential.

6. **Undershoot/Afterhyperpolarization:** In some neurons, the repolarization phase can lead to a brief period of hyperpolarization, where the membrane potential becomes more negative than the resting potential. This is known as the afterhyperpolarization or undershoot.

7. **Refractory Period:** After an action potential, there is a refractory period during which the neuron is temporarily unable to generate another action potential. This period is essential to ensure that action potentials remain discrete and propagate in one direction along the neuron's axon.

Week 1 - lecture 3:- Basic structures in the brain

Major division into cerebrum and cerebellum :-

Cerebrum:

The cerebrum is the largest and most prominent part of the brain, occupying the uppermost portion. It is divided into two hemispheres: the right hemisphere and the left hemisphere. The two hemispheres are connected by a bundle of nerve fibers called the corpus callosum, allowing them to communicate and share information.

Cerebellum:

The cerebellum is located at the back of the brain, just below the cerebrum. Although it is much smaller in size compared to the cerebrum, the cerebellum contains a significant number of neurons and is essential for motor coordination, balance, posture, and fine motor skills.

Sulcus: A groove or indentation on the surface of the brain; it helps increase the surface area for accommodating more brain tissue.

Gyrus: A raised and convoluted ridge on the brain's surface; it plays a role in increasing the brain's surface area to accommodate more neurons.

Fissure: A deep and prominent groove that separates large brain regions; it provides structural divisions and helps organize different brain areas.

Two major furrows—the central sulcus and the lateral sulcus—divide each cerebral hemisphere into four sections: the frontal, parietal, temporal, and occipital lobes. The central sulcus, also known as the fissure of Rolando, also separates the cortical motor area (which is anterior to the fissure) from the cortical sensory area (which is posterior to the fissure). Starting from the top of the hemisphere, the upper regions of the motor and sensory areas control the lower parts of the body, and the lower regions of the motor and sensory areas control the upper parts of the body.

  • Frontal Lobe:

Location:- Located in the front of the brain, behind the forehead.Function:- Responsible for executive functions, decision-making, planning, motor control, personality, and higher-order cognitive processes.

  • Parietal Lobe:
  • -Location: Positioned near the top and back of the brain, behind the frontal lobe.

Function: Processes sensory information related to touch, temperature, pain, and spatial awareness.

  • Temporal Lobe:

Location: Found on the sides of the brain, below the parietal and frontal lobes.Function: Involved in auditory processing, language comprehension, memory, and emotions.

  • Occipital Lobe:

Location: Located at the back of the brain, behind the parietal and temporal lobes.Function: Primarily responsible for processing visual information.

  • Medulla Oblongata:Location: The lowest part of the brainstem, continuous with the spinal cord.Function: Controls essential functions for survival, including breathing, heart rate, blood pressure, and digestion.
  • Pons:

Location: Positioned above the medulla oblongata, part of the brainstem.Function: Involved in sleep regulation, respiratory control, and relaying sensory and motor information between the cerebrum and cerebellum.

Primary Visual Cortex (V1):

Location: Occipital lobe (back of the brain).

Function: Receives and processes visual information from the eyes, allowing us to perceive and interpret the visual world.

Primary Auditory Cortex (A1):

Location: Temporal lobe (side of the brain).

Function: Receives and processes auditory information from the ears, enabling us to perceive and interpret sounds.

Primary Somatosensory Cortex (S1):

Location: Parietal lobe (top and back of the brain).

Function: Receives and processes somatosensory information from the skin, muscles, and joints, allowing us to perceive touch, pressure, pain, and temperature.

Primary Gustatory Cortex:

Location: Insular cortex (inside the brain).

Function: Receives and processes taste information from the taste buds, allowing us to perceive different tastes.

Primary Olfactory Cortex:

Location: Temporal lobe and olfactory bulb (under the front of the brain).

Function: Receives and processes olfactory information from the nose, enabling us to perceive and distinguish different smells.

Week 1:- Lecture 4:- Systems of neural processing

The sensory nervous system is a part of the nervous system responsible for processing sensory information. A sensory system consists of sensory neurons (including the sensory receptor cells), neural pathways, and parts of the brain involved in sensory perception and interoception. Commonly recognized sensory systems are those for vision, hearing, touch, taste, smell, balance and visceral sensation. Sense organs are transducers that convert data from the outer physical world to the realm of the mind where people interpret the information, creating their perception of the world around them.

Transduction:- Transduction refers to the process by which sensory stimuli from the external environment are converted into electrical signals or neural impulses that the nervous system can understand and process

The process of transduction varies depending on the type of sensory system involved. Each sensory system has specialized receptors that are sensitive to specific types of stimuli, such as light for vision, sound waves for hearing, chemical molecules for smell and taste, and mechanical pressure for touch. These receptors are located in various sensory organs, like the eyes, ears, nose, tongue, and skin.

Each sensory system (visual, auditory, somatosensory, olfactory, gustatory) has specialized sensory receptors that are sensitive to specific types of stimuli in the external environment.

Sensory receptors are located in various sensory organs such as the eyes, ears, nose, tongue, and skin.

When sensory stimuli interact with sensory receptors, they trigger biochemical or physical changes within the receptors.

These changes lead to the conversion of sensory stimuli into electrical signals or neural impulses, known as transduction.

Sensory Pathways:-The electrical signals, now in the form of neural impulses, travel along dedicated sensory pathways to the central nervous system (CNS).Each sensory system has specific neural pathways that transmit the sensory information to the CNS.The neural impulses from different sensory pathways reach their corresponding sensory areas in the brain's central nervous system.The CNS processes and interprets the sensory information in specific regions of the brain responsible for each sensory modality.

VISUAL SENSORY PROCESSING


Retina:

The process begins in the retina, the light-sensitive tissue lining the back of the eye.

Specialized photoreceptor cells called rods and cones detect light photons and convert them into electrical signals.

Retinal Pigment Epithelium (RPE):

The RPE is a layer of cells adjacent to the photoreceptor layer.It functions to nourish and support the photoreceptor cells and helps maintain the health of the retina.

Photoreceptor Layer (Rods and Cones):

The photoreceptor layer contains two types of photoreceptor cells: rods and cones.

Rods: Rods are responsible for low-light (scotopic) vision, allowing us to see in dim light conditions. They are highly sensitive to light and provide black-and-white vision.

Cones: Cones are responsible for color (photopic) vision and function best in bright light conditions. They are less sensitive to light but provide high-resolution color vision.

Outer Nuclear Layer (ONL):The ONL contains the cell bodies of the rods and cones.

It serves as a structural and supportive layer for these photoreceptor cells.

Outer Plexiform Layer (OPL):The OPL is the site of synapses between photoreceptors and bipolar cells.It plays a role in transmitting visual signals from photoreceptors to bipolar cells.

Bipolar Cell Layer:

Bipolar cells receive signals from photoreceptor cells and pass them to ganglion cells.

They play an intermediate role in visual signal processing.

Inner Plexiform Layer (IPL):

The IPL is the site of synapses between bipolar cells and ganglion cells.It is involved in further processing and integration of visual signals before they are sent to the brain.

Ganglion Cell Layer:

Ganglion cells are the final output neurons of the retina.They receive visual signals from bipolar cells and transmit them as action potentials through their axons, which form the optic nerve.

Ganglion cells are responsible for carrying visual information from the retina to the brain for further processing and perception.

The pathway from the optic nerve to the primary visual cortex involves several stages of visual processing.

  • Optic Nerve:

The electrical signals from the photoreceptors travel through the optic nerve, which is a bundle of nerve fibers that extends from each eye.

  • Optic Tracts:

After the optic chiasm, the fibers that crossed over and those that remained on the same side continue as optic tracts.

The optic tracts carry visual information from both eyes towards the brain.

  • Lateral Geniculate Nucleus (LGN):

After passing through the optic chiasm, the electrical signals reach the lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN), a thalamic nucleus in the diencephalon.

The LGN acts as a relay station, where visual information is processed and sent to the primary visual cortex.

  • Primary Visual Cortex (V1):

The processed visual information from the LGN is transmitted to the primary visual cortex (V1), located in the occipital lobe at the back of the brain.

V1 is the first cortical area that receives and processes visual input.

It is organized in a topographic map, where adjacent regions in the retina project to adjacent regions in V1, preserving spatial relationships.

  • Extrastriate Visual Cortex (V2, V3, V4, etc.):

From V1, visual information is sent to higher-order visual cortical areas known as the extrastriate visual cortex (V2, V3, V4, etc.).

These areas process more complex visual features, such as object recognition, color perception, and motion processing.

Dorsal and Ventral Pathways:

Within the extrastriate cortex, two major visual processing pathways emerge: the dorsal and ventral pathways.

The dorsal pathway (also called the "where" or "how" pathway) is involved in processing visual information for spatial awareness, motion perception, and guiding actions.

The ventral pathway (also called the "what" pathway) is responsible for object recognition and identifying the identity of objects.

  • Higher-Order Visual Areas:

Beyond the dorsal and ventral pathways, visual information is further processed in other higher-order visual areas.

These areas are involved in more specialized visual functions, such as facial recognition (fusiform face area) and reading (visual word form area).

  • Visual Association Areas:

Visual information from different visual areas is integrated and processed in visual association areas, such as the posterior parietal cortex and the prefrontal cortex.

These areas play a role in higher-level cognitive processes related to visual attention, working memory, and decision-making based on visual stimuli.

It is a critical component of the higher-order visual processing system and plays a crucial role in object recognition, complex visual processing, and categorization. Week 1- Lecture 5-  Methods of Recording Neural Activity

Single unit electrophysiology

  • Single-unit recording involves sampling the activity of single neurons, or small clusters of neurons, using an array of microelectrodes implanted in the brain.
  • Metal electrodes are used to record the spiking activity (action potential ) from single units.
  • An electrode introduced into the brain of  living animals, can record the electrical activity happening near the tip of the electrode.
  • If the tip  is placed near to a single neuron extracellularly , then we get single unit recording
  • The difference in potential in the extracellular space of a neuron with reference to the ground is measured.
  • The recorded action potentials are similar to the action potentials of a cell , but they are much smaller .
  • Microelectrodes are very fine wires made of tungsten or platinum-iridium alloys that are insulated except at the tip of the electrode.
  •   When there are more neurons near the electrode tip, there would be another set of spiking information(different shape or different time ) would also be present in the data
  • With spike sorting techniques, we can differentiate the spiking activity of different neurons .
  • The recordings could be from dendrites, multiple neurons or axons , since we are not sure about the placement of electrodes. The electrode may or maynot capture the spiking activity or a single neuron or more number of neurons, but very limited.

Multiple channel electrodes

  • Multiple channels are present in a single electrode and these are present in different configurations.
  • This allows neuronal populations to be considered during electrophysiology experiments.
  • Drawbacks include damage to brain tissues and more recovery time from the damages.

  • But the major advantage is the data (spiking information ) being acquired from more neurons simultaneously.

Local field Potential

  • The raw signal from the electrophysiology contains high frequency spiking information and low frequency information known as local field potentials(LFP).
  • LFP are "extracellular" signals, meaning that they are generated by transient imbalances in ion concentrations in the spaces outside the cells, that result from cellular electrical activity. LFP are 'local' because they are recorded by an electrode placed nearby the generating cells.

Patch clamp Technique

  • Possible to record from a single neuron to exactly get the voltage difference (Vout- Vin) .[1]
  • Its is extremely difficult to record at this level for human subjects or even in awake behaving animals [2].
  •  Whole-cell recordings can be obtained from awake head-restrained mice [3].
  • Voltage clamps:- Voltage across membrane is kept constant and current through the membrane can be measured.
  • Current clamp :-Current flow is kept constant and voltage across the membrane is measured.

Calcium Imaging

  • From electrophysiology and patch clamp techniques, it's not possible to understand about different properties  and types  of neurons like morphology, and how they connect with one another.
  • During spiking activity there is an influx of calcium into the cell body and amount of calcium influx can be measured.
  • The calcium transients are associated with the spikes
  • With the help of laser scanning microscopes and fluorescent marker dyes(fluorophores), the calcium influx can be recorded.
  • WIth 2 photon microscopes [2], the calcium influx during spiking for a very small region of interest of tissues(in femtoliters) can be measured.
  • Information from 100-1000 neurons can be obtained with different scanning methods available .
  • With different fluorescent proteins like Gcamp, it's possible to mark particular types of neurons like VIP(Vasoactive Intestinal Polypeptide), SOM(Somatostatin), PV(parvalbumin).Its possible to have differential information by different fluorescent proteins from different types of neurons
  • Due to penetration of light and its absorption by tissues involved in 2 photon imaging, there is a limitation with the depth of the brain that can be imaged using this technique.
